Electrical wiring and conduits for residential work

Residential electrical wiring is governed in India by the National Building Code Part 8 and by the National Electrical Code derived from IEC 60364. The cabling, conduit and protection devices that go behind the walls of a single-family house typically last forty to fifty years before any rework, which puts the specification in the same long-life class as the structural concrete and the load-bearing masonry. This article walks through the wiring sizes, conduit choices and protection arrangements that produce a defensible residential installation.

Conduit choices

Concealed-wiring conduits in residential construction are typically uPVC heavy-duty or HMS metal conduit. uPVC heavy-duty conduit under IS 9537 Part 3 is the residential default, with a smooth bore that allows easy pulling, a wall thickness adequate for chase-cutting in masonry and concrete, and elbows and tees with solvent-cement joints. HMS conduit is used where mechanical protection is required, typically in industrial and commercial work or in residential service rooms where conduit is exposed.

The conduit fill ratio should not exceed forty percent for a single cable, thirty-one percent for two cables and forty percent for three or more cables under IEC 60364-5-52, computed as the cross-sectional area of the conductors divided by the internal area of the conduit. Over-filled conduits trap heat, prevent future re-pulling and complicate troubleshooting; under-filled conduits waste material. A 25-millimetre conduit comfortably carries up to six 2.5-millimetre-square cables; a 20-millimetre conduit carries up to four 1.5-millimetre-square cables.

Distribution board layout

A typical residential distribution board has a main isolator at 40 A or 63 A double-pole, a 100 milliamp time-delayed main RCCB for fire protection, and a series of sub-circuits each with its own MCB and where applicable a 30 milliamp Type A RCCB for personal protection. The board should be split into at least two RCCB groups, typically a wet-area group covering bathroom and kitchen sockets and an dry-area group covering bedrooms and lighting; this prevents a single trip from blacking out the whole house.

Critical loads such as the refrigerator, the inverter or UPS, the WiFi router and selected lighting should be on a separate group that does not share an RCCB with the wet-area group. The single most common DB layout error in residential work is the use of one main RCCB for the whole house, which produces a complete blackout on any earth-leakage event in any wet area; the fix is two or more RCCB groups with selectivity verified against the manufacturer time-current curves.

Earthing

The protective earth conductor must be sized per IEC 60364-5-54 Table 54.2, which gives the PE conductor as the same size as the phase conductor for phase sizes up to 16 millimetres square, half the phase size for sizes above 16 up to 35, and 25 millimetres square for sizes above 35 to 50. The PE wire is identified by green-and-yellow insulation and is bonded to the earth electrode at the main DB.

The earth electrode for a residential service is typically a copper-bonded steel rod of 2.5 to 3 metres length driven into permanently moist soil, with an earth resistance test under IS 3043 showing a value below five ohms in most domestic services. Where the soil is rocky or sandy and a single rod cannot achieve five ohms, a multi-rod array or a chemical earth electrode is used. The earth resistance should be measured at commissioning and recorded in the project file.

Surge protection

Surge protective devices under IEC 61643-11 are increasingly specified on residential installations in lightning-prone regions of South India. A Type 2 SPD installed at the main DB protects against indirect lightning strikes and switching surges with a let-through voltage typically below 1.5 kilovolts. The SPD must have a backup overcurrent device per the manufacturer specification, typically a 63 A gG fuse, to protect the SPD itself from end-of-life short-circuit failure.

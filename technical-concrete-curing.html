Concrete curing: methods, duration and common errors

Curing is the single highest-leverage item in the concrete lifecycle and the most commonly neglected on Indian construction sites. This article sets out the curing requirements under ACI 308, IS 456 Clause 13.5 and ASTM C1074 maturity method, with specific guidance for hot-weather conditions typical of Tumakuru, Bengaluru, Hyderabad, Chennai, Coimbatore and the wider South Indian construction belt.

Why curing matters

Fresh concrete reaches its design strength through the continued hydration of cement by water over a period of weeks. If the water content falls below the level needed for hydration, either through evaporation or through absorption by dry formwork and subgrade, the cement ceases to react and the concrete never reaches the strength the mix design targeted. A slab that is not cured for the first seven days loses roughly thirty percent of its potential twenty-eight-day compressive strength, and the surface zone that carries the structural load in a flexural member loses even more. Curing also reduces plastic and drying shrinkage, controls thermal cracking by moderating the rate of temperature rise, and improves durability by reducing the permeability of the cover zone.

Duration

IS 456 Clause 13.5.2 requires a minimum of seven days of continuous curing for ordinary Portland cement concrete and fourteen days for concrete containing mineral admixtures such as fly ash, ground granulated blast-furnace slag or silica fume. ACI 308 recommends seven days for Type I cement, fourteen days for Type II through Type V and for blended cements, and twenty-one days for mass concrete elements. The European standard EN 13670 specifies curing duration as a function of surface temperature, concrete development class and exposure class, with the total curing duration ranging from twelve hours for rapid-hardening concrete at thirty degrees Celsius up to more than ten days for slow-hardening concrete in aggressive exposure at five degrees.

Worked example: hot-weather M30 slab in Tumakuru

Consider a 200 millimetre thick M30 slab of 500 square metres poured in April when the ambient air temperature at site peaks at 39 degrees Celsius and the relative humidity falls to 30 percent in the afternoon. The mix uses Portland Pozzolana Cement with twenty percent fly ash, with a water-cement ratio of 0.45 and a twenty-eight-day target strength of 30 N per square millimetre.

The rate of evaporation under these conditions, computed from the Menzel formula or equivalently from the ACI 305 nomograph, reaches approximately 1.2 kilograms per square metre per hour in the two hours after placement if no protective measures are taken. This exceeds the one kilogram per square metre per hour threshold above which plastic shrinkage cracking becomes likely. The mitigation measures under ACI 305 are to pre-cool the mix water with ice, to apply a fog spray to the surface immediately behind the finishing operation, to install wind breaks if the wind speed exceeds three metres per second and to apply an evaporation retarder before any finishing passes. Curing should begin no later than two hours after finishing by means of wet hessian continuously kept damp for fourteen days.

Under the ASTM C1074 maturity method, strength gain is tracked by computing the maturity index as the integral of temperature over time with respect to a datum temperature, typically zero degrees Celsius for Indian practice. An in-place maturity sensor logs the time-temperature history and correlates it against a laboratory-calibrated strength-maturity curve for the specific mix. At an average in-place temperature of 32 degrees Celsius, the M30 mix typically reaches 70 percent of its twenty-eight-day strength by day seven and full strength by day twenty-one, giving a basis for formwork stripping and post-tensioning decisions without waiting for cube test results.

Common curing errors

The single most common error on Indian sites is stopping curing after three or four days, usually because the labour contractor has moved on to the next pour and the site supervisor has no independent mandate to continue the watering regime. The preventive measure is a written curing schedule issued by the project engineer at the start of every pour, with daily sign-off by the site supervisor and random audit by the structural consultant. A second common error is curing with salt-contaminated or silt-laden water, which introduces chlorides and fines into the surface zone and compromises durability; potable water must be used. A third error is premature removal of formwork before the concrete has reached the stripping strength, typically 70 percent of design strength; this causes sag, cracking and long-term deflection that cannot be recovered.

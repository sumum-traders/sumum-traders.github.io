For homeowners and small-project buyers

A residential distribution board does three jobs: switch the circuits on and off, trip on overload or short circuit, and disconnect on an earth leakage that could cause shock. The MCB handles the overload and short-circuit protection; the RCCB handles the earth-leakage protection; a combined RCBO does both in a single unit.

MCB rating and curve selection follows the load. Lighting and fan circuits use 6 or 10 amp curve B MCBs on 1.5 sq.mm copper. General socket circuits use 16 amp curve C on 2.5 sq.mm copper. Dedicated circuits for geysers, air-conditioners and cookers use 20 amp curve C on 4 sq.mm copper. Curve B suits resistive loads and long lighting runs; curve C suits mixed general power; curve D is reserved for high-inrush motor and transformer loads. Oversized breakers on undersized cables are the most common reason residential wiring overheats and eventually burns.

RCCB selection follows the load electronics. Every home with induction cookers, LED drivers, variable-speed fans, EV chargers or electronic appliances needs Type A RCCBs at 30 mA sensitivity; the older Type AC cannot sense pulsating DC leakage from these modern loads and will fail to trip on a real earth fault. Install the RCCB upstream of each group of final circuits, and split the house into at least two RCCB groups so that a single trip does not black out the entire property.

Wire sizing follows the breaker rating and the run length. Lighting circuits on 6 or 10 amp breakers need 1.5 sq.mm copper. Socket circuits on 16 amp breakers need 2.5 sq.mm copper. Dedicated heavy-load circuits on 20 or 25 amp breakers need 4 sq.mm copper. For runs longer than 20 metres add one size to manage voltage drop; undersized wires on long runs cause voltage drop, flickering lights and appliance failure. Use stranded copper, not solid, for flexibility during installation, and always use full-copper wire rather than copper-clad aluminium.

Modular switches and sockets are the final fit-out. The Indian standard is a 1-module, 2-module or 3-module plate hosting individual switches, sockets, fan regulators and USB charging ports. Choose a single brand family for the entire house because plate and frame compatibility is brand-specific; mixing brands inside a single plate is visually and functionally unreliable.

For contractors and site engineers

Electrical installation design on a commercial or institutional project is governed by IEC 60364, which covers low-voltage electrical installations from fundamental principles through selection and erection. The standard is organised into seven parts, with Part 4 on protection for safety, Part 5 on selection and erection of equipment, Part 6 on verification and Part 7 on requirements for special installations and locations.

Protective device selection follows the cable current-carrying capacity under IEC 60364-5-52 and the short-circuit prospective current at the installation point under IEC 60909. The design current must not exceed the protective device rating, and the protective device rating must not exceed the cable derated current. For a standard 16 amp final circuit on 2.5 sq.mm copper with four grouped conductors at 35 degrees ambient, the cable derated capacity falls to 14.66 amps, which means the cable must be upsized to 4 sq.mm or the grouping broken; this is the most common sizing error at site and is the single highest-leverage item in a design review.

RCCB type selection under IEC 61008-1 has moved decisively against Type AC in commercial specifications. Type A is the minimum for any circuit feeding electronic loads, Type F adds single-phase variable-frequency drive currents, and Type B adds smooth DC residual currents required for three-phase VFDs, DC-side EV charging and transformerless PV inverters. Specifying Type AC for a modern commercial installation is a code violation in jurisdictions that reference IEC 60364-4-41 updates post-2017.

SPD selection under IEC 61643-11 is driven by the lightning protection zone concept under IEC 62305. Type 1 SPDs are installed at the origin of the installation where direct lightning strikes are possible; Type 2 SPDs are installed at sub-distribution boards; Type 3 SPDs are installed close to sensitive equipment. Coordination between types and with the upstream overcurrent protection must follow the manufacturer selection chart; an uncoordinated SPD can nuisance-trip the incomer or fail short during a lightning event.

Enclosure IP ratings under IEC 60529 become critical in wet and dusty environments. IP20 is suitable for indoor office and residential use; IP44 is suitable for bathroom splash zones and general outdoor light-duty; IP54 or IP55 is required for outdoor equipment and industrial settings; IP65 and above is specified for wash-down and rain-exposed outdoor installations. Specifying IP20 equipment for a building exterior is a visible failure within one monsoon.

Governing standards referenced in this category are IEC 60898-1 for MCBs, IEC 61008-1 for RCCBs, IEC 61009-1 for RCBOs, IEC 60364 series for low-voltage installations, IEC 60529 for IP ratings, IEC 61643-11 for SPDs and IS 694 for Indian wire and cable.

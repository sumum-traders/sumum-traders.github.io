Paint, putty and primer: the correct sequence on cementitious walls

The single largest cause of paint failure on Indian residential walls is incorrect sequence of putty, primer and paint on freshly plastered cementitious substrates. The substrate has to be cured, dried and pH-stabilised before any putty or primer is applied; the putty has to be sanded between coats; the primer has to be re-applied after the putty; and the topcoats have to be applied with the right interval. This article walks through the correct sequence step by step, with cross-references to ASTM D4258 for surface preparation and ASTM D7091 for film thickness measurement.

Step one: substrate cure and dry

Cement plaster reaches its working pH below ten only after fourteen to twenty-one days of curing followed by seven to ten days of drying. A freshly plastered wall has a surface pH above twelve, which saponifies any organic paint binder applied to it, leading to peeling, blistering and staining within months. The wall should be tested with pH paper at multiple points before any priming is undertaken; if the reading is above ten, further drying is required. Most paint failures attributed to substrate quality, climate or workmanship are actually pH-driven failures from premature painting.

The moisture content of the substrate at the time of priming should be below eight percent, measured with a moisture meter. The wall should be dry to touch, with no damp patches at corners or behind permanent fixtures. Painting in the first monsoon after construction is almost always premature; the moisture trapped behind paint surfaces eventually pushes through and causes flaking.

Step two: alkali-resistant primer

The first coat on a cured and dried plaster should be an alkali-resistant primer based on acrylic emulsion, applied at the manufacturer-specified spreading rate, typically eight to ten square metres per litre on a smooth substrate. The primer seals the porosity of the plaster, reduces the residual alkali penetration, and provides a uniform substrate for the putty. A water-thinned dilution of the topcoat itself is not a substitute for an alkali-resistant primer; the diluted topcoat does not contain the alkali-resistance chemistry.

Step three: putty in two coats with sanding

Wall putty is applied in two thin coats over the primed plaster, with sanding between coats to eliminate brush marks and trowel ridges. The first coat is approximately one to one and a half millimetres thick after sanding, applied with a steel trowel in upward strokes, sanded with 220-grit paper after twelve to twenty-four hours of drying. The second coat is approximately half a millimetre thick after sanding, applied perpendicular to the first coat for full coverage, sanded with 320-grit paper. The total putty thickness should not exceed two millimetres; thicker applications are prone to delamination and shrinkage cracking.

Acrylic putty such as Asian Paints TruCare cures by water evaporation and is suitable for both interior and exterior surfaces. White-cement putty such as JK Wall Putty and Birla White cures by hydration and is suitable for interior surfaces; on exterior walls a white-cement putty without polymer modification can develop hairline shrinkage cracks. The choice between the two on interior walls is largely a question of brand preference and price.

Step four: second coat of primer over putty

A second coat of alkali-resistant primer is applied over the cured putty before the topcoats. This step is frequently skipped on residential projects to save cost, but it is the difference between a paint job that lasts five years and one that lasts ten. The putty surface is highly absorbent and uneven in absorption; the primer evens out the absorbency so that the topcoats lay down with consistent sheen and colour. Skipping this step typically produces patchy sheen and visible roller marks within the first year, especially on south-facing exterior walls.

Step five: two topcoats with correct interval

The first topcoat of emulsion paint is applied at the manufacturer-specified spreading rate, typically twelve to fourteen square metres per litre, with a quality roller. The second topcoat follows after four to six hours of drying for water-based emulsions and twenty-four hours for solvent-based enamels. The two topcoats together should achieve the manufacturer-specified dry film thickness, typically forty to sixty microns for interior emulsions and sixty to ninety microns for exterior emulsions. Dry film thickness can be measured with a magnetic gauge and should be verified at multiple points on a project of any scale.

Hot-weather considerations

South Indian summers from March through May produce surface temperatures on south and west facing walls that can exceed forty-five degrees Celsius by mid-afternoon. Painting on a hot substrate causes rapid solvent flash-off, leaving the binder unflowed and producing brush marks, lap marks and reduced bond. The recommended approach is early morning or late afternoon application during the hottest months, working on shaded walls in the late afternoon and on east-facing walls in the early morning. The rule of thumb is that the substrate temperature should not exceed thirty-five degrees Celsius during application.

Procurement checklist for BOQ to PO

Procurement of building materials at any scale beyond a single house benefits from a written checklist that walks the buyer through the steps from BOQ scrubbing to purchase order issuance. The checklist below is the working version Sumum Traders LLP uses to engage with institutional buyers, contractors and project consultants, and is offered here as a template that can be adapted to specific project formats.

Step 1: BOQ scrubbing

The first step is to scrub the bill of quantities so that every line is unambiguous, complete and procurable. Lines such as cement at six thousand bags or steel at fifty tonnes are common but not procurable as written, because they specify neither grade nor brand-class nor delivery schedule. A procurable BOQ line specifies the grade, the standard reference, the unit rate basis, the delivery schedule and the acceptance criterion. Lines that fail any of these tests should be flagged for clarification with the consultant before any quotation is sought.

Step 2: supplier longlist and shortlist

The supplier longlist for any category should be drawn from BIS-licensed manufacturers and their authorised distributors. Direct sourcing from the manufacturer is preferred where order size justifies it; distributor sourcing is the norm for residential and small commercial projects. The longlist should be reduced to a shortlist of three to five suppliers per category by applying screening criteria covering BIS licence validity, GSTIN status, financial standing, delivery footprint to the project pincode and reference checks from comparable past projects. A supplier without a verifiable BIS licence on the relevant standards should not be on the shortlist regardless of price.

Step 3: request for quotation

The RFQ document sent to the shortlisted suppliers should carry the project identifier, the BOQ lines for which quotations are sought, the delivery schedule, the quality plan reference, the payment terms and the response deadline. The RFQ should not name a target price or a competitor quote; doing so converts the exercise from price discovery to price confirmation. The response deadline should be at least seven working days for residential projects and at least fourteen working days for institutional projects, to allow the suppliers to verify availability and obtain mill quotes where applicable.

The lowest price is rarely the right choice. The right choice is the lowest qualified price, where qualification means the supplier has been verified on BIS licence, GSTIN, delivery capability and reference checks. A bid that is fifteen percent below the median typically signals either a misunderstanding of the specification or a supplier in financial distress; in either case the procurement risk is higher than the price saving justifies.

Step 5: purchase order issuance

The purchase order is the contractual instrument that authorises the supplier to deliver. The PO should reference the RFQ and the accepted quotation, repeat the specification and quantity in full rather than by reference, state the unit rate and total value, state the GST rate and amount, define the delivery schedule and location, define the payment terms and the basis for any liquidated damages, and identify the buyer-side authorised signatory. The PO is signed by the buyer and acknowledged in writing by the supplier before any despatch.

Step 6: receipt, inspection and reconciliation

On receipt the goods are inspected against the PO specification, the quantity is verified, and the goods receipt note is prepared and signed. The four-way match between PO, tax invoice, e-way bill and GRN is performed before payment release. Any discrepancy is logged and resolved with the supplier in writing. Once the four-way match is clean, the payment is released per the PO terms, and the file is closed.

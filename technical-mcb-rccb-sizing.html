MCB and RCCB sizing, trip curves, discrimination and coordination

This guide walks through miniature circuit breaker and residual current circuit breaker sizing against IEC 60364, IEC 60898 and IEC 61008, with cable coordination, short-circuit verification per IEC 60909 and surge protection per IEC 61643. From our Kyathsandra yard in Tumakuru we deliver the same technical support to contractors across Bengaluru, Chennai, Hyderabad, Coimbatore, Kochi and Mysuru.

Five rules that decide most switchboards

  1. Size the cable for the derated current first, then pick an MCB whose rated current sits between design current and cable capacity, never the other way around.
  2. Use curve B for pure resistive loads, curve C for mixed general power, and curve D only when motor or transformer inrush genuinely exceeds ten times rated current.
  3. Apply 30 mA Type A RCCBs on every socket and wet-area final circuit, step up to 100 or 300 mA time-delayed for upstream fire protection, and reserve 10 mA for medical or critical lighting.
  4. Verify short-circuit withstand at both ends of the cable so the breaker’s I²t let-through stays below the conductor’s k²S² limit under IEC 60364-4-43.
  5. Coordinate the SPD and the upstream MCB together — an uncoordinated Type 2 SPD will nuisance-trip the incomer or fail short during a lightning surge.

For homeowners and small-project buyers

If you are wiring a flat, a small shop or a two-storey house, the distribution board has three jobs. It must switch power off safely, it must trip quickly on a fault, and it must disconnect on an earth leakage before you feel a shock. The MCB handles overload and short circuit. The RCCB handles earth leakage. Neither device substitutes for the other, and a single combined RCBO only saves panel space, not protection logic.

Start by listing every circuit: lights, fan points, 6 A sockets, 16 A sockets, geyser, air-conditioner, kitchen appliances, pump. Each circuit needs its own MCB. A typical lighting circuit uses a 6 A or 10 A curve B MCB on 1.5 mm² copper. A general socket circuit uses a 16 A curve C MCB on 2.5 mm² copper. A 15 A geyser or air-conditioner point uses a dedicated 20 A curve C MCB on 4 mm² copper. Oversized breakers on thin cable are the most common reason a house burns down after a minor fault — the cable cooks long before the breaker notices.

The RCCB is the life-safety device. Install one 30 mA Type A RCCB upstream of every group of final circuits that feeds sockets, bathrooms, kitchens, outdoor points or any appliance with electronics. Type AC is obsolete for modern homes because induction hobs, LED drivers, variable-speed fans and EV chargers produce pulsating DC leakage that a plain Type AC device cannot sense. Split the house into at least two RCCB groups so a single trip does not black out the whole property, and keep critical lighting, the refrigerator and the router on separate groups from wet areas.

Do not mix brands inside a single distribution board unless you have checked the busbar pitch and the breaker width. Pick one OEM ecosystem per board — Schneider Electric, Siemens, ABB, Havells, Legrand Myrius, Anchor Panasonic or similar — and stay within it.

Finally, ask your contractor for a one-page schedule that lists each MCB rating, each RCCB sensitivity, the cable cross-section and the circuit length. That sheet is the difference between a board you can maintain in ten years and a box of mystery switches. If the contractor cannot produce it, the sizing was probably guessed.

For contractors and site engineers

Load schedule, demand factors and design current

Every sizing exercise begins with a load schedule prepared under IEC 60364-3. The design current IB is the connected load multiplied by a diversity factor appropriate to the occupancy: typically 0.6 to 0.8 for residential lighting and sockets, 1.0 for fixed continuous loads such as HVAC, and 1.25 for the largest motor in a bank to account for its starting contribution. Apply power factor and simultaneity separately; do not fold them into a single fudge factor.

The nominal current of the protective device In must satisfy IB ≤ In ≤ Iz, where Iz is the cable’s continuous current-carrying capacity after derating. The second condition, I2 ≤ 1.45 · Iz, is automatically met by IEC 60898 MCBs because their conventional tripping current I2 is 1.45 times In. This is not true for all IEC 60947-2 MCCBs, where I2 can reach 1.3 In and must be checked explicitly.

Grouping factors bite hardest in India because conduit runs routinely carry four to six loaded conductors. Four loaded conductors in one conduit give a grouping factor of 0.65, six give 0.57. Ambient correction at 35 °C on PVC is 0.94, at 40 °C drops to 0.87, and at 45 °C reaches 0.79. A 2.5 mm² cable rated 24 A at B1 collapses to 14.7 A under four-conductor grouping at 35 °C, which is below a 16 A MCB and therefore non-compliant unless the conductor is increased to 4 mm² or the grouping is broken.

MCB trip-curve selection under IEC 60898-1

IEC 60898-1 defines three instantaneous tripping bands for residential and light-commercial MCBs. Curve B trips between 3 and 5 times In, curve C between 5 and 10 times, and curve D between 10 and 20 times. Industrial devices under IEC 60947-2 offer adjustable magnetic settings and are marked K, Z or MA for motor protection variants.

RCCB sensitivity and type selection under IEC 61008-1 and IEC 61009-1

IEC 61008-1 covers pure RCCBs, IEC 61009-1 covers RCBOs. The residual current waveform the device can detect is defined by the type letter. Type AC senses only sinusoidal AC leakage and is no longer acceptable for modern loads. Type A adds pulsating DC with smooth components up to 6 mA. Type F covers mixed frequencies from single-phase variable-frequency drives. Type B detects smooth DC residual currents and is mandatory for three-phase frequency converters, DC-side EV charging and PV inverters without galvanic isolation.

Short-circuit and discrimination

Prospective short-circuit current at the origin of the installation is derived from the source impedance using IEC 60909. For a typical 11 kV / 433 V distribution transformer of 500 kVA at 5% impedance, the three-phase fault at the LV terminals is approximately 13.3 kA. The MCB’s rated short-circuit capacity Icn (6 kA or 10 kA for domestic IEC 60898 devices, up to 25 kA for industrial IEC 60947-2) must exceed the prospective fault at the point of installation. Equally important is the cable adiabatic check from IEC 60364-4-43: the breaker’s let-through I²t must stay below k²S² for the conductor, where k is 115 for PVC-insulated copper.

SPDs under IEC 61643-11 and IEC 61643-12 must carry a maximum continuous operating voltage Uc at least 1.1 times the nominal phase voltage, and the backup fuse or MCB must be at or below the manufacturer’s declared value, typically 63 A gG per IEC 60269.

Common sizing and coordination mistakes

  1. Picking the MCB rating from the appliance nameplate and then running whatever cable fits the conduit, instead of deriving cable size from the derated current.
  2. Using curve D for ordinary induction motors, which delays fault clearance on the feeder cable and can fail the adiabatic I²t check.
  3. Installing a Type AC RCCB on circuits feeding LED drivers, induction hobs, variable-speed pumps or EV chargers.
  4. Fitting a single 30 mA RCCB as the main incomer for the whole house, so one earth leakage blacks out everything.
  5. Ignoring grouping derating when four or more circuits share a conduit.
  6. Assuming cross-brand discrimination between upstream and downstream breakers without consulting the OEM selectivity table.
  7. Installing a Type 2 SPD without the manufacturer-specified backup fuse.

Plumbing pipes: CPVC versus uPVC versus SWR

Residential plumbing in South India has standardised on three plastic-pipe families: CPVC for hot-and-cold water supply, uPVC for cold-water supply and pressure mains, and SWR for above-ground soil, waste and rainwater drainage. Each family has its own standards, joint methods, pressure ratings and acceptable applications, and using one in place of another, even though it is mechanically possible, produces premature failure within a few years. This article walks through the three families and the choice between them.

CPVC for hot-and-cold supply

Chlorinated polyvinyl chloride pipe under ASTM F441 and F442 is the right choice for hot-and-cold water supply in any residential or commercial plumbing system. CPVC is rated for continuous service at temperatures up to 93 degrees Celsius and pressures up to 28 bar at 23 degrees Celsius, with derating curves at higher temperatures. Joints are made by solvent cement under ASTM F493, and a properly cemented joint is stronger than the pipe itself.

CPVC is supplied in two sizing systems. SDR 11 to copper-tube-size dimensions is the predominant standard in India and aligns with international CPVC fittings. The schedule 80 dimension system is used in industrial and commercial applications. Within a single project all CPVC pipe and fittings should be from the same dimension system; mixing is a source of joint failure.

uPVC for cold supply and pressure mains

Unplasticised polyvinyl chloride pipe under IS 4985 and ASTM D1785 is the right choice for cold-water supply, pressure mains, agricultural irrigation and underground service connections. uPVC is rated for continuous service at temperatures up to 60 degrees Celsius. The pipe is supplied in pressure classes from 2.5 kg per square centimetre to 12.5 kg per square centimetre, identified by the wall thickness and the colour code on the pipe stencil.

Joints in uPVC are made by solvent cement for sizes up to 50 millimetres and by rubber-ring elastomeric joints for sizes above 50 millimetres. The choice of joint method on larger sizes is driven by thermal movement; rubber-ring joints accommodate axial movement of the pipe under temperature change, which is significant on long runs of underground supply main. Solvent cement on a long underground run produces compressive stress that eventually fractures fittings.

SWR for soil, waste and rainwater drainage

Single-stack soil-waste-and-rainwater pipe under IS 13592 is the right choice for above-ground drainage, including soil pipes from WC to manhole, waste pipes from kitchen and bathroom fittings, and rainwater downpipes from roof gutters to ground. SWR is a non-pressure system and is not rated for water supply; it carries flow under gravity at near-atmospheric pressure. Joints are made by rubber-ring elastomeric seal in the socket of the pipe; solvent cement is used only for permanent connections and at fittings that require positive sealing.

SWR is supplied in two types under IS 13592. Type A is for ventilation and rainwater applications without sustained chemical exposure; Type B is for soil and waste applications with sustained chemical and biological exposure. The pipe wall thickness and the resin formulation differ between the two types, and substituting Type A for Type B in a soil application produces premature softening and brittle failure.

The PPR alternative for hot-and-cold supply

Polypropylene random copolymer pipe under DIN 8077 and DIN 8078 is an alternative to CPVC for hot-and-cold water supply, with joints made by heat fusion rather than solvent cement. PPR is rated for higher temperatures than CPVC and produces a permanently fused joint that is mechanically homogeneous with the pipe. PPR is more common in commercial and institutional projects and in regions where heat-fusion welding is well-established; CPVC is more common in residential projects in India because the solvent-cement joint is faster, lower-skill and more familiar to plumbers.

Common installation errors

The single most common installation error on CPVC supply lines is dry-fitting without solvent cement, leaving the joint to seal by friction alone. The joint will leak within months and the eventual repair cost outweighs the install saving by an order of magnitude. The fix is mandatory solvent cement at every joint per the manufacturer specification, with cure time before pressure testing.

The second common error is mixing CPVC and uPVC fittings on the same line. The dimension systems are similar but not identical, and a CPVC fitting on a uPVC pipe or vice versa will leak under thermal cycling even if it appears to fit at install. Single-material runs from source to delivery point are mandatory.

The third common error is undersized SWR vent stacks. The vent stack diameter should equal at least half the soil-stack diameter for adequate atmospheric balance; under-sized vents produce siphoning of trap seals and sewer-gas ingress to occupied space. The plumbing consultant should size the vent against the fixture-unit count under the National Plumbing Code or the equivalent project specification.

Pressure testing

Every plumbing line should be hydraulically pressure tested before concealment. CPVC and uPVC supply lines are tested at 1.5 times the working pressure for at least one hour with no pressure drop. SWR drainage lines are tested by water-fill to the highest fixture for at least thirty minutes with no leakage at any joint. The test results should be recorded with the pressure-gauge reading at start and end and the duration; a tested system that fails should not be concealed.

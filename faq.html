Frequently asked questions

Thirty questions covering product selection, grade specification, delivery logistics, documentation, payment and returns for the full eight-category stock at the Kyathsandra yard. The answers are written to be usable by homeowners, contractors and institutional buyers alike, and are cross-referenced to the relevant category landing pages and technical articles throughout the site.

1. Which cement grade should I use for a single-house construction in Tumakuru or Bengaluru?

For most single-house work of two or three storeys, Portland Pozzolana Cement or Ordinary Portland Cement 43 grade is sufficient across footings, columns, slabs and plaster. OPC 53 is worth the extra cost only where the structural engineer has specified it for high-stress elements such as long-span beams or transfer slabs. For coastal sites in Mangaluru, Kochi or Kozhikode, PPC or PSC is preferred for durability under chloride exposure.

2. What is the difference between Fe 500 and Fe 500 D TMT rebar, and which should I buy?

The letter D after the grade number indicates a ductile variant with guaranteed higher elongation and tighter limits on carbon, sulphur and phosphorus chemistry. Fe 500 D is the correct default for any structure in seismic zones II or III, which covers all of Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Telangana and coastal Andhra Pradesh. The price premium over plain Fe 500 is typically two to four percent, which is a very small share of total cost and buys a meaningful safety margin.

3. How much cement do I need for one cubic metre of M20 concrete?

A thumb rule that works reliably for small jobs is six to seven bags of cement per cubic metre of M20 concrete, which corresponds to approximately 320 kilogrammes of cement. A more precise mix design will account for the specific aggregate sources and the target slump, and is always preferable for structural work.

4. What is the shelf life of cement in bags?

A sealed bag of cement stored on a dry pallet in a covered shed retains its full strength for approximately ninety days from the manufacturing date printed on the bag. After ninety days the hydration reactivity begins to decline, and after six months the bag should be rejected for structural use regardless of appearance. Bags that have gone lumpy from moisture cannot be restored and should be rejected immediately.

5. Which tile adhesive class should I use for vitrified tiles on a wet area floor?

A C2 or C2TE S1 adhesive under EN 12004 or IS 15477 is the correct class for vitrified tiles in a wet area. The C2 designation indicates improved bond strength above 1 N per square millimetre, the T suffix confirms slip resistance for wall application, the E suffix extends open time, and the S1 suffix adds deformability needed for large-format tiles over a cementitious substrate. A C1 adhesive is not adequate for vitrified tile because the low water absorption of the tile body prevents adequate bond.

6. How long should I cure a freshly poured concrete slab?

IS 456 Clause 13.5.2 requires a minimum of seven days of continuous curing for Ordinary Portland Cement concrete and fourteen days for concrete containing fly ash, slag or silica fume admixtures. In hot weather conditions typical of South Indian summers, curing should be extended by a further three to seven days and must be continuous rather than intermittent. A slab that is not cured for the first seven days loses roughly thirty percent of its potential twenty-eight-day compressive strength.

7. What is the correct sequence for paint, putty and primer on a new cement plaster wall?

On a fully cured and dried cement plaster, apply an alkali-resistant primer as the first coat once the plaster pH is below ten. Follow with two thin coats of acrylic or white-cement wall putty, sanded between coats to eliminate brush marks. Apply a second coat of primer over the putty to seal absorbency, then two topcoats of emulsion paint with four to six hours between coats. Skipping the primer or applying putty over uncured plaster is the most common cause of paint peeling within the first year.

8. When should I use CPVC versus uPVC pipes in residential plumbing?

CPVC is the correct material for hot-and-cold water supply, rated for continuous service up to 93 degrees Celsius and pressures up to 28 bar. uPVC is the correct material for cold-water supply, soil, waste and vent lines, rated for continuous service up to 60 degrees Celsius. PPR is used in commercial and institutional hot-water applications where fusion welding is preferred over solvent cement. SWR is a non-pressure drainage system used exclusively for above-ground soil and waste lines.

9. What sensitivity of RCCB should I install on a residential distribution board?

A 30 milliamp Type A RCCB is the correct default for every circuit feeding sockets, wet-area appliances and outdoor points. Type AC is obsolete for modern homes because induction cooktops, LED drivers, variable-speed fans and electric-vehicle chargers produce pulsating DC leakage that a Type AC device cannot sense. Install separate RCCBs for wet-area circuits and dry-area circuits so that a single trip does not black out the entire home.

10. What trip curve should I specify for a ceiling-fan and LED lighting circuit?

Curve B under IEC 60898 is appropriate for circuits dominated by resistive or near-resistive loads such as traditional lighting, fans and heaters, and trips between three and five times the rated current on short-circuit. Curve C is the correct default for mixed general power including socket outlets, small motors and modern LED lighting banks with switched-mode drivers; it trips between five and ten times the rated current. Curve D is reserved for high-inrush loads such as transformers, welders and large motors.

11. How do I know if a TMT bar at site is genuinely Fe 500 D?

Every BIS-licensed TMT bar carries rolling marks embossed on the surface that repeat every metre or so, showing the manufacturer identifier, the grade designation such as 500 D and often the cast number. The bundle tag carries the mill test certificate reference and the BIS licence number. On receipt, the diameter and mass per metre should be checked against IS 1786 Table 1 for three random bars per size per consignment, and a tensile test under IS 1608 and bend test under IS 1599 should be performed at an NABL lab for one sample per fifty tonnes.

12. Is a GST invoice necessary for every sale to a contractor or builder?

A GST invoice is mandatory for every business-to-business sale where the buyer holds a GSTIN and seeks to claim input tax credit. The invoice must carry the supplier GSTIN, the buyer GSTIN, a unique invoice number in a single series, the HSN code for each line item, the taxable value, the GST rate and amount, and the supplier signature or digital signature. For sales above the statutory threshold the invoice must be registered on the Invoice Registration Portal to obtain an Invoice Reference Number and a QR code.

13. What is an e-way bill and when is it required?

An e-way bill is an electronic document generated on the GST e-way bill portal for the movement of goods worth more than fifty thousand rupees, and is required before the goods leave the supplier premises. The e-way bill carries a unique number that must accompany the truck, and is valid for a period based on the distance to the destination, typically one day per two hundred kilometres. Movement without a valid e-way bill is subject to detention and penalty under the GST laws.

14. How does the yard handle site-access constraints such as narrow roads or low entrances?

Every enquiry should include a note on the site access conditions, including the approach road width, any low-clearance points, the availability of unloading labour and whether a forklift or crane is needed. Based on this information the yard selects the appropriate truck size, typically eight or twelve tonnes for constrained access and sixteen or twenty-five tonnes for open access. A site-survey photograph attached to the enquiry helps the yard size the vehicle correctly on the first attempt.

15. Does the yard accept returns on unopened cement bags or unused tiles?

Returns are considered on a case-by-case basis and are typically accepted for unopened and undamaged stock within seven days of delivery, with a handling deduction to cover freight and restocking. Cement that is older than ninety days from the manufacturing date cannot be accepted for return because it will not carry full strength in a resale. Cut-and-bend rebar, custom-tinted paint and cut-to-length pipe are not returnable.

16. What is the typical delivery lead time from Tumakuru to Bengaluru or Mysuru?

Same-day or next-morning despatch is standard for destinations within a hundred and fifty kilometres of Tumakuru, which covers Bengaluru, Mysuru, Hassan and Chitradurga. For destinations within three hundred kilometres, next-day to two-day despatch is standard, which covers Hubballi-Dharwad, Mangaluru, Coimbatore and Salem. For destinations beyond five hundred kilometres, two-to-four-day despatch is standard, depending on freight consolidation.

17. What is the minimum order quantity for cement and steel?

There is no formal minimum order quantity; the yard accepts orders from ten bags of cement or one hundred kilogrammes of TMT steel upward. However, deliveries below one tonne total load are priced on a part-load basis and may be consolidated with other orders to a common destination pincode, which can delay the despatch by one or two working days.

18. What payment methods are accepted?

Bank transfer to the firm account is the standard payment method, with account details provided on the proforma invoice. Cheque payments are accepted subject to realisation before despatch. Cash payments are discouraged and are not accepted for institutional orders or for amounts exceeding the statutory threshold. UPI transfers are accepted for small retail sales.

19. Does the yard supply materials to sites outside the twenty-six listed cities?

The twenty-six cities listed as the regular service footprint are the principal destinations served on a standing freight corridor. The yard also despatches to smaller towns and villages within the same states on a case-by-case basis, subject to freight economics and minimum load. An enquiry to the yard email with the destination pincode returns a feasibility confirmation within one working day.

20. How does the yard verify the authenticity of a brand such as Asian Paints or UltraTech?

Every pack or bag sold at the yard is sourced directly from an authorised distributor of the manufacturer and carries the manufacturer batch code, the BIS licence number where applicable and the manufacturing date. The yard retains purchase invoices from the distributor as traceability evidence and can share these with the buyer on request for institutional orders.

21. What is the difference between a tax invoice and a proforma invoice?

A proforma invoice is a pre-transaction document issued by the seller to confirm the terms of a proposed sale, including specification, quantity, unit rate, GST and delivery terms; it is not a statutory document and does not create a GST liability. A tax invoice is the statutory GST document issued after the goods are despatched, and triggers the GST liability and the input tax credit for the buyer. The yard issues the proforma on PO receipt and the tax invoice on despatch.

22. Does the yard provide material test certificates with steel and cement?

Yes, mill test certificates for TMT rebar and manufacturer test certificates for cement are issued with every consignment on request and are mandatory for institutional orders. The certificate carries the cast or batch number, the chemical analysis, the mechanical test results and the declaration of conformity to the relevant Indian standard. A buyer requiring third-party testing at an NABL lab can arrange it at site and the yard will facilitate sampling.

23. Is there a showroom where I can see samples before ordering?

The Kyathsandra yard carries a physical sample counter for tiles, paints, pipes and electricals, and a full-size display for sanitaryware and selected fittings. Samples can be viewed by appointment during office hours Monday through Saturday. For bulk selections where colour matching is critical, small mock-up panels can be prepared at the site before the order is finalised.

24. What happens if the delivered material does not match the specification?

The buyer should record the deviation on the goods receipt note at the time of delivery, retain the delivery challan and take photographs of the consignment. The yard accepts the return and replaces the material at no cost, provided the notification is received within forty-eight hours of delivery. For institutional orders with a formal goods inward inspection, the inspection window runs for seven days from delivery.

25. Can the yard supply materials against a tender-specified brand alternative?

Where a tender specifies a named brand or equivalent, the yard can supply the named brand where available or propose a BIS-licensed alternative with equivalent or better performance parameters. The alternative proposal is submitted in writing with the manufacturer test certificate and the standards compliance, and is subject to the consultant approval.

26. Does the yard undertake installation or only material supply?

The yard is a materials supplier and does not undertake installation, contracting or project execution. For installation requirements, the yard can refer buyers to authorised installers of the relevant brand, such as certified tile-adhesive applicators for Laticrete or waterproofing applicators for Dr Fixit, but the installation contract is between the buyer and the installer.

27. How does the yard handle damaged goods in transit?

The yard despatches every consignment with photographic evidence of loading and the truck condition. If damage is observed on delivery, the buyer records the damage on the goods receipt note, retains photographic evidence, and notifies the yard within twenty-four hours. Claims are settled by replacement of the damaged material at no cost, with the yard bearing the freight loss.

28. Is the yard open on Sundays and public holidays?

The yard and office are open Monday through Saturday from 6 AM to 9 PM and are closed on Sunday. Despatches scheduled for Monday morning are typically loaded on Saturday afternoon and held for early-Monday departure. Emergency despatches on Sunday can be arranged with prior notice at a premium freight rate, subject to truck availability.

29. What standards are the tiles supplied by the yard compliant with?

Glazed vitrified and ceramic tiles supplied by the yard conform to ISO 13006 for ceramic tile classification, with test methods under the ISO 10545 series, and are additionally cross-referenced to ANSI A137.1 and EN 14411 as required by the project specification. The DCOF slip-resistance value under ANSI A326.3 is specified for wet-area floors and is available on the manufacturer data sheet.

30. How can I request a site visit or a technical consultation from the yard?

The yard provides technical consultations by telephone or email for any active order. On-site visits are arranged by request for institutional orders above a threshold order value, and for builder accounts on a recurring basis. An email to the yard email with the site address and the scope of the consultation will receive a response within one working day with a scheduled date.

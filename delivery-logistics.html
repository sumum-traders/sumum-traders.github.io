Delivery and logistics: from yard to site without surprises

Delivery is where most building-material orders go wrong. The truck arrives at a site that cannot accept it, the documentation does not match the consignment, the unloading labour is unavailable, the e-way bill expires en route or the buyer-side check fails because no one was briefed in advance. This article walks through the delivery and logistics workflow from yard despatch to site reception, covering vehicle sizing, documentation, e-way bills and the buyer-side preparation that turns a delivery into a routine event rather than a crisis.

The right vehicle for an order is the smallest that fits the load and the access. Oversized vehicles ordered to demonstrate capacity often cannot reach the site, requiring a transhipment that adds half a day and unrecoverable cost. Undersized vehicles ordered to save freight require multiple trips and over-utilise the unloading labour. The yard sizes the vehicle on the basis of the destination pincode, the access constraints declared by the buyer and the consolidated order size; a single accurate access photograph from the buyer changes the choice on roughly one in five orders.

Documentation that travels with the truck

The documentation set on a typical despatch includes the tax invoice, the delivery challan, the e-way bill, the loading sheet from the yard, the weighbridge slip and the driver lorry receipt. The tax invoice and the e-way bill are the regulatory documents required for the goods movement. The delivery challan accompanies the load when the supply is on consignment or stock-transfer basis where the tax invoice is issued later. The loading sheet, the weighbridge slip and the driver receipt are internal documents that close the chain of custody from yard to truck.

The driver carries the original tax invoice and the e-way bill in physical form for inspection at any tax checkpoint en route. A digital copy on a phone is not currently accepted at all checkpoints, despite the regime being electronic in principle, so the physical print travels with the load.

E-way bill validity and route

The e-way bill is generated immediately before the truck departs the yard and is valid for one day per two hundred kilometres of route distance for normal cargo. A consignment from Tumakuru to Bengaluru of approximately seventy kilometres has a validity of one day; a consignment to Chennai of approximately three hundred and fifty kilometres has a validity of two days. If the validity expires en route, the bill must be re-validated by the supplier with a fresh validity period before the truck moves further; running on an expired bill is treated as movement without a bill and attracts penalty.

The route declared on the e-way bill is the point-to-point road route, not a multi-stop or detour route. If the truck deviates significantly from the declared route, for example to drop a part-load at an intermediate destination, the bill must be amended or a fresh bill must be generated for the second leg. Sumum Traders LLP plans the route at the time of e-way bill generation and avoids multi-stop dispatch on consignments where the regulatory exposure is higher than the freight saving.

Buyer-side preparation

A site that is ready to receive a consignment has four things in place. First, an unloading area with sufficient room for the truck to park alongside, ideally with reverse access. Second, an unloading labour gang of three to six workers depending on load type and weight, briefed on the expected arrival window. Third, a designated receiver, normally the site engineer or storekeeper, who will sign the delivery challan and prepare the goods receipt note. Fourth, the receiver brief, which is a one-page sheet listing the expected items, quantities and any specification checks to perform on receipt.

Bagged cement should be unloaded to a covered storage area on wooden pallets, separated from earth contact, with the manufacturing date visible on the bag for batch tracking. TMT bars should be unloaded to a wooden-sleeper rack, separated by diameter and grade, with the bundle tags retained on the bundle. Tiles, paint and electricals should be unloaded to a lockable indoor store. Pipes should be stored on level ground out of direct sunlight to prevent UV degradation of plastic.

What goes wrong, and how to avoid it

The single most common delivery failure is a missing or unbriefed receiver at site. The truck arrives, no one is empowered to sign for the consignment, and the driver waits or returns. The fix is a written confirmation from the buyer twenty-four hours before the despatch window, naming the receiver and the mobile number. The second common failure is short labour at site, which forces the driver to wait beyond the demurrage allowance or refuse to unload. The fix is to size the labour gang against the load weight and to brief the gang on the expected arrival window. The third common failure is access mismatch, which the access photograph normally catches, but which occasionally surfaces because of an obstruction or a road closure that emerged after the photograph was taken; in such cases the driver calls the buyer for an alternative drop-point.
